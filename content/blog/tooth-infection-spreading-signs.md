A toothache is a dental problem. A spreading tooth infection is a medical one. The distinction matters, because while a [dental abscess](/blog/dental-abscess-antibiotics-uk) confined to the tooth and its immediate surroundings is common and treatable at the dental surgery, an infection that escapes those boundaries and spreads into the surrounding tissues can become genuinely dangerous.

This isn't said to alarm. It's said to inform. The vast majority of dental infections are straightforward: they hurt, they get treated, they resolve. But understanding what a spreading infection looks like, and when to stop waiting for a dental appointment and start thinking about urgent medical care, is knowledge that can prevent a rare but serious complication from becoming a crisis.

## How Dental Infections Start

An infection in a tooth follows a predictable pathway. Bacteria enter the tooth's internal space through decay, a crack, or trauma. The pulp (nerve tissue) becomes infected and dies. The bacteria multiply in the dead tissue and spread through the root tip into the surrounding bone. An abscess forms: a pocket of pus at the root tip, surrounded by inflamed bone and tissue.

At this stage, the infection is localised. It's confined to the tooth and the bone immediately around it. The body's immune system walls it off, creating a barrier of inflammatory tissue that contains the infection. The symptoms are a [toothache](/blog/toothache-at-night) (often severe), tenderness when pressing on the tooth, and possibly a small swelling on the gum.

This is the dental appointment stage. [Root canal treatment](/blog/root-canal-treatment-cost-uk) or [extraction](/blog/tooth-extraction-cost-uk) removes the source of infection. [Antibiotics](/blog/dental-abscess-antibiotics-uk) may be prescribed to support the body's response. The infection resolves.

The problem arises when the infection overwhelms the body's containment. The abscess expands beyond the bone, breaches the barriers, and spreads into the soft tissue spaces of the face and neck. These spaces, fascial planes that connect anatomical compartments, provide pathways for infection to travel rapidly.

## The Warning Signs of Spreading Infection

These signs indicate that a dental infection has moved beyond the tooth and is involving surrounding tissues.

### Rapid Facial Swelling

Some swelling around an abscessed tooth is normal: a localised puffiness near the tooth, confined to the gum or the cheek immediately adjacent. This is contained infection, and while it needs treatment, it's not an emergency in the same way.

Spreading infection produces swelling that's different in character: rapid (developing over hours rather than days), extensive (involving a large area of the face, under the jaw, or into the neck), firm or hard to the touch (not soft and fluctuant like a localised abscess), warm, and red.

If the swelling is pushing the eye closed, extending below the jawline into the neck, or causing visible distortion of the face on one side, the infection has entered deeper tissue spaces and needs urgent attention.

### Difficulty Swallowing

This is a red flag that demands immediate action. The floor of the mouth and the tissues around the throat are connected to the dental spaces by direct fascial planes. An infection from a lower tooth (particularly lower molars and wisdom teeth) can spread into the submandibular and sublingual spaces beneath the tongue and floor of the mouth.

When these spaces fill with infection, the tongue pushes upward and backward, the floor of the mouth swells, and swallowing becomes progressively difficult. The medical term for this presentation is Ludwig's angina, and it's a genuine emergency because the swelling can progress to compromise the airway.

If someone with a dental infection reports difficulty swallowing, a feeling that the tongue is swelling, or an inability to open the mouth fully (trismus), they need emergency department assessment.

### Difficulty Breathing

The most serious warning sign. If infection in the floor of the mouth or throat progresses enough to compromise the airway, breathing becomes laboured, noisy, or difficult. This is a 999 situation. Airway compromise from a dental infection is rare, but when it occurs, it can deteriorate rapidly.

Any change in breathing quality, including noisy breathing (stridor), shortness of breath, or a feeling of tightness in the throat, in the context of a dental infection requires emergency medical care immediately.

### Fever

A localised dental abscess typically doesn't produce significant fever. The infection is contained, and the systemic response is limited.

A spreading infection triggers a systemic immune response: fever (temperature above 38°C), chills, sweating, elevated heart rate, and a general feeling of being unwell. The body is fighting an infection that's no longer localised, and the systemic signs reflect that escalation.

A fever above 38.5°C in conjunction with dental swelling indicates the infection is spreading and needs urgent treatment: antibiotics, drainage, or both.

### Malaise and Confusion

Feeling generally unwell, fatigued, or confused in the context of a dental infection suggests sepsis: the body's overwhelming response to infection that can affect multiple organ systems. Sepsis from dental infection is rare but represents a genuine medical emergency.

Signs of sepsis include: confusion or altered mental state, rapid breathing, rapid heart rate, feeling very unwell or "the worst I've ever felt," cold and clammy skin despite fever, and reduced urine output. Any of these, in combination with a dental infection, require emergency department attendance.

## Where Infections Spread

The anatomy of the head and neck creates predictable pathways for dental infection to travel.

**Upper teeth** infections can spread upward into the cheek (buccal space), toward the eye (orbital space), or backward toward the temple (temporal space). An infection approaching the eye produces swelling around the orbital rim and can, in rare cases, affect vision.

**Lower teeth** infections most commonly spread downward and inward: into the submandibular space (beneath the jaw), the sublingual space (under the tongue), and the parapharyngeal space (alongside the throat). These are the pathways to Ludwig's angina and airway compromise.

**Lower wisdom teeth** deserve specific mention because their position at the back of the lower jaw provides direct access to the parapharyngeal space. [Wisdom tooth infections](/blog/pericoronitis-treatment-uk) and [impacted wisdom tooth](/blog/impacted-wisdom-teeth-uk) abscesses are the most common dental infections that spread into dangerous territories.

The path from "dental abscess" to "medical emergency" runs through these anatomical spaces. The progression can be gradual (over days) or rapid (over hours), which is why monitoring a worsening dental infection is important.

### Urgent Dental or A&E Level

If you have spreading facial swelling, fever, or difficulty opening the mouth (trismus), seek urgent care. If a dental surgery is available, an urgent dental appointment provides drainage and antibiotics. If dental care isn't immediately available (weekends, nights), A&E can provide IV antibiotics, imaging, and surgical drainage.

### 999 / A&E Immediately

If you have difficulty breathing, difficulty swallowing, confusion, or signs of sepsis, go to A&E or call 999. These symptoms indicate the infection has progressed beyond what dental treatment alone can manage. Hospital treatment includes IV antibiotics, imaging (CT scan to identify the extent of spread), and potentially surgical drainage under general anaesthetic.

## The Timeline

Most dental infections progress slowly enough that there's time to get dental treatment before they become dangerous. A cavity develops over months. The pulp infection develops over weeks. The abscess forms over days. The typical patient has had symptoms for some time before the infection reaches a critical point.

But occasionally, the progression is faster. Patients who are immunocompromised (diabetes, HIV, chemotherapy, long-term steroid use) have reduced ability to contain infections, and spread can occur more rapidly. Patients who take anti-inflammatory medications (ibuprofen, steroids) may mask the early signs of spreading infection, allowing it to progress further before being detected.

The key observation is trajectory. An infection that's improving (less pain, less swelling) is being managed successfully by the immune system and/or antibiotics. An infection that's worsening (more pain, more swelling, new symptoms) is spreading despite the body's efforts. A worsening trajectory demands escalation of care.

## Prevention

Prompt treatment of dental infections, before they have time to spread, is the most effective prevention. The tooth that's been [aching for weeks](/blog/toothache-at-night), the abscess that's been treated with repeated courses of antibiotics without definitive dental treatment, the [wisdom tooth infection](/blog/pericoronitis-treatment-uk) that keeps coming back: these are the scenarios where delay creates risk.

Antibiotics without source control (treatment of the infected tooth) are a temporary measure. They suppress the infection but don't eliminate it. The bacteria remain in the tooth, and when the antibiotic course ends, the infection returns, sometimes more aggressively because the sensitive bacteria have been killed while resistant ones survive.

Definitive treatment, [root canal](/blog/root-canal-treatment-cost-uk) or [extraction](/blog/extracting-infected-tooth-uk), removes the source. The infection resolves because the bacteria no longer have a home.
